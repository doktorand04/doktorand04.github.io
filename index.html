About My Research

My research focuses on understanding the intricate dynamics of ecological and evolutionary processes through mathematical modeling and analysis. I work at the intersection of dynamical systems theory and biology, developing mathematical frameworks to capture the complexity of adaptive dynamics and eco-evolutionary mechanisms.

I am particularly interested in how populations evolve in response to environmental pressures and ecological interactions, and how these evolutionary changes feed back into ecological dynamics. This bidirectional coupling between ecology and evolution creates rich mathematical structures that I explore using tools from nonlinear dynamics, statistical mechanics, and differential equations.

Through my work, I aim to provide rigorous mathematical foundations for understanding phenomena such as adaptive diversification, evolutionary branching, and the emergence of complex ecological patterns from simple evolutionary rules.

About Me

I am a doctoral student at Umeå University in Sweden, where I work on mathematical approaches to ecology and evolution. My academic journey reflects an interdisciplinary path, starting with a background in chemistry before transitioning to the mathematical sciences.

This transition from molecular-scale modeling of chemical systems to population-level dynamics of living organisms has given me a unique perspective on how mathematical tools can bridge vastly different scales of biological organization. I bring computational techniques and quantitative rigor from my chemistry background to tackle fundamental questions in evolutionary biology.

Beyond research, I am passionate about the beautiful interplay between abstract mathematics and the natural world, always seeking to uncover the elegant mathematical principles that govern biological complexity.
